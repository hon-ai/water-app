Mara stepped through the door. Outside, snow. Inside, dust.

She moved fast. She moved fast. Her hand on the latch. Her hand on the lamp. Her hand on the desk drawer that should have been locked and was not. The drawer slid open without sound. Inside, a letter, opened, refolded, opened again. The paper had been handled. The handwriting was not hers.

She read the first line. She read the second. She set the letter on the desk and stepped backward to the window, hand still flat where the letter had been, as if she could keep reading without looking. Outside the snow was falling sideways now, the way it did when the wind shifted off the cove. The lamp guttered. The room was very quiet. She did not move for what felt like minutes but could not have been more than seconds; the lamp was still alive.

The east wing of The Pell Library held no rooms anyone called by name. The lower stacks where she had spent her childhood with Aren held no light at all in the deeper passages. She knew every cabinet. She knew which floorboards lifted. She knew where the brass lanterns hung and where they did not. She had once known.

A boy named Theo stood at the end of the corridor, hands in his pockets, watching her. She had not heard him. Theo was supposed to be at the harbor with Aren and the others. Theo was looking at her the way a stranger looks at someone they have just decided about.

"Aunt Mara?" Theo said.

The merchants in the harbor square were trading silk for olive oil. The autumn ledgers ran thin. The cove froze over for the first time in eleven years. None of this had anything to do with the letter or the boy or the room she stood in, except that all of it was happening at once, and Mara was supposed to be holding it together.

She smiled at Theo and pulled the desk drawer closed with her hip. She told him to come over. Her voice was warm. She had practiced the warmth that morning, walking up the cliff path with the wind at her back, and the practice held.

He came. She bent and held his cold hand and felt the small bones of it and thought, This child is mine to keep alive, and the room around her became, in that instant, a place she would never tell anyone the truth about. The lamp settled. The letter on the desk did not move. The snow continued falling, sideways, beautiful, indifferent.
